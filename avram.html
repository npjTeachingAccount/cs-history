Henriette Avram

Dates: October 7, 1919 - April 22, 2006

Significant Contribution

Henriette's most outstanding achievement was almost single-handedly creating the MARC system. MARC (Machine-Readable cataloging) is a method of converting paper-and-ink catalogs into digital catalogs and changed how librarians handled cataloging country-wide as it was implemented as a standard in 1971. Even though she was not a librarian, her contributions to the field through her computer science approach resulted in her receiving almost every award in the area. She died from cancer, a disease she once had dreamt of curing, on April 22, 2006. She is buried next to her husband in Arlington National Cemetery in Arlington, Virginia

Biography

Henriette Regina Davidson Avram was an American programmer and was responsible for the creation of the MARC program. She was born in 1919 in New York City as a part of the Jewish community and spent much time in her youth at libraries. She attended Hunter College for two years in the pre-medicine field and had dreams of curing cancer, which had been a big part of her family history. At 21, she cut her studies to marry Herbert Avram, a Navy Lieutenant and chess player. In 1952, Herbert took a job at the NSA, and after a few years of studying math, so did Henriette. There, she became a member of the pioneering generation of computer programmers, a very select group of people. For a woman to be a part of that group in the 50s was extremely rare. They were the only people in the world to program on a computer then. Henriette Avram had many significant feats during her lifetime.
